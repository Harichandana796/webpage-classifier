<div class="Section1">
<p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;text-align:center"><b style="mso-bidi-font-weight:normal"><span style='font-size:14.0pt;font-family:
"Eras Medium ITC"'>Deployment of Conference XP over Wireless Network:<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span><o:p></o:p></span></b></p>
<p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;text-align:center"><b style="mso-bidi-font-weight:normal"><span style='font-size:14.0pt;font-family:
"Eras Medium ITC"'>Summarized Test Results<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>
<p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;text-align:center"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";mso-bidi-font-weight:bold'>Adam Eck, LD
Miller, and Leen-Kiat Soh<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;text-align:center"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";mso-bidi-font-weight:bold'>{aeck, lmille,
lksoh}@cse.unl.edu<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;text-align:center"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>256 Avery Hall, <st1:place w:st="on"><st1:city w:st="on">Lincoln</st1:city>, <st1:state w:st="on">NE</st1:state> <st1:postalcode w:st="on">68588-0115</st1:postalcode></st1:place><o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p align="center" class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;text-align:center"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>Tel: (402) 472-6738<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">   </span>Fax: (402) 472-7767<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><b><span style='font-family:
"Eras Medium ITC";color:blue'>1.<span style="mso-tab-count:1">       </span>Brief
Introduction<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>In
the following, we report on a series of tests on deploying Conference XP (CXP)
over a wireless network here at the <st1:place w:st="on"><st1:placetype w:st="on">University</st1:placetype>
 of <st1:placename w:st="on">Nebraska</st1:placename></st1:place>.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>There are four phases of tests:<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="disc">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l10 level1 lfo1;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><b><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>Phase
     I</span></b><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>:<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span><b>Preliminary</b> tests, in April 2006,
     were conducted to determine the drop rate of the existing wireless network
     when using Conference XP to chat and send presentation slides<o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l10 level1 lfo1;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><b><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>Phase
     II:<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>PGM Implementation</span></b><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'> tests, in July-August 2006, were
     conducted when implementing the PGM protocol to replace the UDP protocol
     for Conference XP<o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l10 level1 lfo1;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><b><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>Phase
     III:</span></b><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'><span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span><b>PGM Deployment</b> tests, in
     September 2006, were conducted to determine how the PGM-powered Conference
     XP would work in a real classroom, with 30+ laptops competing for wireless
     access points in a classroom.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l10 level1 lfo1;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><b><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>Phase
     IV:</span></b><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'><span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span><b>I-MINDS Deployment</b> tests, in October
     2006, were conducted to determine how the CXP-powered I-MINDS would work
     in a real classroom over wireless, with 30+ laptops.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Note that we have extensively tested
     I-MINDS deployed in wired networks.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
     </span>Indeed, I-MINDS has been deployed at the online <st1:place w:st="on"><st1:placename w:st="on">Bellevue</st1:placename> <st1:placetype w:st="on">University</st1:placetype></st1:place>
     since May 2006.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>Overall,
the above tests were successful in terms of providing us insights to continue
to refine and revise our designs and implementations.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Phase I tests confirmed Microsoft Research’s
observations of the wireless CXP deployment’s drop rate performances.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Phase II tests validated our PGM
implementation, albeit in a small wireless environment.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Phase III tests verified the correctness of
our PGM implementation, in a classroom-size wireless environment, with 30+
venue participants simultaneously.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
</span>However, Phase IV tests were not as successful as initially hoped as we
realize that we still have to first address specific installation problems with
I-MINDS to minimize potential user errors and database tracking problems. From
analyzing the logs, we have some solution ideas in mind.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We will continue to revise our solutions and
continue with the Phase IV tests in the near future.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><b style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";color:blue'>2.<span style="mso-tab-count:1">       </span>Phase I:<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
</span>Preliminary Tests<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
mso-bidi-font-weight:bold'>These tests confirm the poor drop rate of CXP over
the wireless networks, the better-performance of 802.11g over 802.11b, and chat
messages suffer fewer drops than Powerpoint slides.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><b><span style='font-family:
"Eras Medium ITC"'>In summary</span></b><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>,
the Chat capability was very much better than the Classroom Presenter in terms
of drop rates.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>The Classroom Presenter,
with just one slide, then it was good.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
</span>If there were 2 or more slides, the transmission seemed to get significantly
worse.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>In
<b>summary</b>, the G-connection had better results, rarely encountering drops
of slide pages.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>The B-connection fared

</span>We have sent the event log to Tim Chou for review.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>In
<b>summary</b>, we see that the B-connection fared worse.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><b style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";color:blue'>3.<span style="mso-tab-count:1">       </span>Phase II:<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
</span>PGM Implementation Tests<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
mso-bidi-font-weight:bold'>These tests were conducted when PGM was being
implemented.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We used these tests to
design our PGM implementation, to evaluate our late-join feature and statistics
logging feature.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="disc">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l6 level1 lfo3;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
     mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Overall this system seems to work pretty
     well.  It still doesn’t have the advantage of the old system where
     all receiving could be done on one socket, but at least I do not have to
     manage multiple threads anymore.  With a little bit of error
     handling, the test application seems pretty robust.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="circle">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l6 level2 lfo3;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Message delivery seemed to be pretty
      reliable.  None were dropped.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l6 level2 lfo3;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Interference didn’t seem to be too much of a
      problem either.  At one point, interference seemed to make it so the
      Tablet PC could only receive messages from itself.  However, about a
      second later it suddenly received all the messages it missed from the
      other machines.  They obviously weren’t in the correct order, but at
      least they were received!  This is a huge improvement over
      UDP.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l6 level2 lfo3;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>This was a small test of only 3 machines on
      my local router, but the results are promising.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l6 level2 lfo3;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>The biggest concern is still scalability,
      since the asynchronous methods still do a little thread handling in the
      runtime environment.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>
</ul>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="disc">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l5 level1 lfo4;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
     mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Chat didn’t drop any messages, Local Screen
     Streaming worked, and Shared Browser worked.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l5 level1 lfo4;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
     mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>We also loaded up Presenter and ran a test
     similar to what we initially did back in March/April.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>But now, all 3 machines received the
     inks without dropping a single one!  The latency for all of this was
     very fast as well.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l5 level1 lfo4;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
     mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Finally, our I-MINDS capabilities were loaded and
     they worked flawlessly as well.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l5 level1 lfo4;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
     mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Obviously, this test scenario is pretty
     isolated since it was only 3 machines (2 over wireless) behind a simple
     home router, but the results are very promising.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="disc">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level1 lfo5;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
     mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>During the ~3 minutes (185.475 seconds) of
     testing, 2030 PGM packets were sent back and forth between the three
     machines.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="circle">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level2 lfo5;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Of these 2030 packets, 48 with NAK packets
      (negative acknowledgement – meaning the machine recognized that it missed
      a packet).  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level2 lfo5;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Each NAK was responded to with a NCF packet
      from the original sender, letting the NAK-sender know that they would
      resend the packet, and then an RDATA packet was sent containing the
      original message.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level2 lfo5;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>There were absolutely no NAKs that did not
      have a resulting NCF and RDATA packet, meaning every time someone thought
      they lost a packet, they received it later.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level2 lfo5;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>There were a couple instances where a machine
      had to send several NAKs for a lost packet, but it always received them
      in the end, and each NAK was responded to (these responses were just lost
      like the original packets). <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level2 lfo5;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Given these numbers, there were 943 ODATA
      (original data) packets sent between the machines, resulting in only 48
      NAKs, which is an initial success rate of 94.90%.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level2 lfo5;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>The NAK packets themselves had a 100% rate of
      resolution.  These are very good numbers.  Of course, they will
      be lower in an implementation with more interference and machines, but
      hopefully the drop off isn’t too severe.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level1 lfo5;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
     mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>After analyzing the data for dropped packets, we
     looked for trends in how the machines received the packets.  <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="circle">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level2 lfo5;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>First of all, while PGM does not guarantee
      overall ordering of packets, it does preserve the order from each
      individual sender.  For example, say the three computers each sent
      out packets in the following order (desktop1, laptop1, tablet1, desktop2,
      laptop2, tablet2, desktop3, laptop3, tablet3).  The packets might
      not show up in that exact order at each receiver, but the packets from
      each sender will show up in order (meaning desktop2 will never come
      before desktop1, but tablet3 might come before laptop3). <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l0 level2 lfo5;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
      mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>The other interesting thing is that this
      ordering scheme can result in some pretty interesting things.  For
      example, say my tablet dropped a packet from my laptop. While it was
      waiting to receive that packet after sending a NAK, it would continue
      receiving from itself and my desktop, as one might expect.  What is
      interesting here is that once it finally received the dropped packet, it
      suddenly printed out and logged all the messages sent by the laptop since
      the dropped packet.  In other words, the logs looked something like
      this: desktop47, laptop46 (last packet received before dropped one),
      tablet50, desktop48, tablet51, desktop49, desktop50, tablet52, laptop47
      (received copy of the dropped packet), laptop48, laptop49, laptop50,
      laptop51.  This sudden rush of packets from one sender was common
      after every single NAK.  This is kind of nice because it preserves
      order for that sender and it doesn’t penalize that sender by making every
      subsequent packet be processed later – it tries to “catch-up.”<o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>
</ul>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>In <b>summary</b>, we are ready to begin testing in
a larger environment with more machines.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><b style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";color:blue'>4.<span style="mso-tab-count:1">       </span>Phase III:<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>PGM Deployment Tests<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
mso-bidi-font-weight:bold'>Now we were comfortable with our local tests, we
wanted to test the PGM deployment at a real classroom.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We requested the IS (Information Services –
our IT group) department to enable the multicast for us at Kauffman Hall where
the class JDE183H is held.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>The results
of these tests were mixed.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We observed
several problems that were not observed in our previous tests.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We also observed some erratic user behaviors
that caused the deployment to fail.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We
also went through a difficult time installing the PGM functionality, the
I-MINDS capabilities, and a database driver, as student laptops had their own
different setups.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We also had to fix the
security updates by Windows.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Please see
Appendix A for our installation guides for the students in JDE183H.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span><span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>But
in the end, we collected enough data to say that PGM has been successfully
deployed.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">   </span><o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>This was our first “<b>real</b>” test.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We asked the students to install the PGM
functionality on their laptops (see <b>Appendix A1</b>). We tried running a
test of 30+ laptops in the classroom running PGM, but we ran into a big
problem: <o:p></o:p></span></p>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="disc">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l7 level1 lfo9;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
     mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>Many of the students were in the venue, but
     not everyone could see each other.  Worse yet, there were no distinct
     groups of people visible across machines (i.e., it wasn’t like half the
     people were visible to their half, and the other half to theirs).  </span><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'><o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>We found out later, after analyzing the logs and
thorough off-site testing, that a Windows security update (KB919007) caused the
PGM implementation to fail.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>After
testing with a packet sniffer again, we determined that NAKs were never being
responded to by senders with NCFs or RDATA packets.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>This caused the receivers to become stuck in
an infinite loop requesting the dropped packet, causing the participants to
time out.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Uninstalling the security
update fixed the problem.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>However,
thanks to Tim, we have been in contact with two members of the Windows security
team and they are working on a solution.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'>In <b>summary</b>, overall, things are looking very
promising and we are looking forward to running more extensive tests.  </span><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>One problem we encountered during this
test was student laptops <b>going into hibernation</b>.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>When this happened the PGM sockets were
closed by the operating system and it was difficult to restore them when the
laptop came out of hibernation.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>In fact
the students would have to close CXP completely and restart it to get back into
the Venue.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">   </span>We asked the students to
change their powersave options, but it is difficult to coordinate such a change
in this large of an environment.<o:p></o:p></span></p>
<span style='font-size:12.0pt;font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";mso-fareast-font-family:
"Times New Roman";mso-bidi-font-family:"Times New Roman";mso-ansi-language:
EN-US;mso-fareast-language:EN-US;mso-bidi-language:AR-SA'><br clear="all" style="mso-special-character:line-break;page-break-before:always"/>
</span>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
mso-bidi-font-family:Arial'><o:p> </o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><b style="mso-bidi-font-weight:
normal"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";color:blue'>5.<span style="mso-tab-count:1">       </span>Phase IV:<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
</span>I-MINDS Deployment Tests<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC";
mso-bidi-font-weight:bold'>We were not too sure about how I-MINDS would work
over wireless networks.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>I-MINDS makes
extensive use of a database, has numerous tracking/logging points, and involves
a large volume of message exchanges.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
</span>Thus, we built a simple simulator to test message sending and
receiving.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>After we were comfortable
with the design, we tried to deploy I-MINDS in the same JDE183H class.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>The deployment was a failure.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We report here our findings.</span><b style="mso-bidi-font-weight:normal"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'><o:p></o:p></span></b></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>To
test the 3.0 version of our capability we installed it on eight borrowed
laptops.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Six of the laptops are six year
old IBM Thinkpads with 802.11b, while the other two were newer Toshiba
Satellite tablets with 802.11g.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>The
laptops were split into two groups.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
</span>Messages sent by participants in the first group were only received by
other participants in the first group.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
</span>The same was true of the second group; there was no overlap between
groups.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span><o:p></o:p></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>On
the teacher version only, we also automatically started the I-MINDS capability.<span style="mso-bidi-font-weight:bold"><o:p></o:p></span></span></p>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="disc">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l9 level1 lfo12;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>More than
     thirty of the students were able to run the script and connect to the UNL
     CXP Venue.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span><o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l9 level1 lfo12;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>Unfortunately
     at this point in test we started to encounter problems.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Around one quarter of the students
     received an exception when CXP tried to load the capability instance for
     their computers.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span><o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l9 level1 lfo12;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>When we
     consulted the error logs for the ConferenceXP API we discovered that these
     students were unable to connect to the I-MINDS database.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>In all but two cases it appeared that
     the students configured the database connection through a system DSN on
     their machines properly.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>However,
     deleting the DSN and creating a new one solved the capability crash
     problem in all but one machine.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>We
     are not sure whether this was just user error, or a more insidious problem
     involving our database connection on a saturated wireless network.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l9 level1 lfo12;
     tab-stops:list .5in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>We also
     had problems with the capability being started more than once.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>This of course caused the capability
     form to appear multiple times on all machines connected to the Venue. <span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> </span>Right now we have two possible
     explanations for why this happened:<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
     </span><o:p></o:p></span></li>
<ul style="margin-top:0in" type="circle">
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l9 level2 lfo12;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>The
      first is that one or more curious students decided to start the
      capability, to see what would happen.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
      </span>While this might have happened once or twice, it does not explain
      the six plus versions of capability running at the same time! <o:p></o:p></span></li>
<li class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt;mso-list:l9 level2 lfo12;
      tab-stops:list 1.0in"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>The
      second explanation is a problem with late join in PGM.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Basically, whenever a user joins a
      venue that already has a capability running, the machine that began the
      capability sends out information to the new user to help them to start
      running the capability as well.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes"> 
      </span>However, late join might conflict with this approach.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Here is how late join works:<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>all senders keep track of their last X
      messages (where X is configurable) in a send window.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>When a new user joins, their receiver
      asks the sender for a certain percentage of those X messages in the
      window (this percentage ranges from 0 – 75).<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>If the original capability start is one
      of those messages in the send window, the user will automatically receive
      the original start, and also the one the sender transmits when the new
      user joins, causing two instances of the capability to begin on the new
      user’s machine.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Since they now
      have two, the second one might cause the new user to become a capability
      sender themselves, which would result in everyone else loading a second
      instance as well.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>Obviously, as
      more users join the venue, this effect will cause even more capability
      instances to be loaded.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span>This
      behavior was observed in our last test in Soh’s class, but we have been
      unable to confirm if it was caused by late join or if students simply
      started up new instances after being told not to.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">   </span><o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>
</ul>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom:6.0pt"><span style='font-family:"Eras Medium ITC"'>In
<b>summary</b>, we need to address the above problems that occur when we scale
up and when we deal with users in non-control environments.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">  </span><o:p></o:p></span></p>
